# Health Strategy and the Tradeoffs We Avoid Naming

In class this week we kept using the word "strategy" like everyone meant the same thing. But I am realizing health strategy is less about having the smartest policy idea and more about deciding which tradeoff you are willing to own in public.

Every option has a cost:
- Expand coverage quickly and you might lose political support from groups who fear higher taxes.
- Protect provider margins and you might preserve access in the short term, but lock in high prices.
- Tighten eligibility and you might show fiscal discipline, while increasing churn for people whose income changes month to month.

None of these choices are neutral. What feels like a technical design decision is usually a values decision wearing a spreadsheet.

The strategic mistake I keep seeing is that teams try to avoid conflict by promising everyone everything: lower cost, better quality, faster implementation, no disruption, no losers. That is messaging, not strategy. Real strategy is sequence and focus. Who do we help first? What do we delay? What political capital are we saving for later?

I am also learning that timing can matter more than policy elegance. A modest reform with a ready coalition can outperform a perfect reform with no institutional home. In health policy, the "best" idea often dies if no agency can implement it, no provider group can tolerate it, and no legislator wants to defend it in an election year.

So for me, health strategy is becoming a discipline of explicit choices:
- Name the target population clearly.
- State the primary objective and what we are deprioritizing.
- Build an implementation path that survives contact with real institutions.
- Define what acceptable failure looks like before rollout.

If we cannot say what we are willing to trade away, we probably do not have a strategy yet. We just have aspirations.
